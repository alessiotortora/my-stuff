# Animations & Microinteractions

Good motion is what makes a web app feel alive — closer to a native experience than a document. The hard part isn't the animation itself; it's making it feel intentional, smooth, and quiet enough that you almost don't notice it's there.

Most interfaces are still mostly static. A little motion in the right place — a button that responds, a sheet that slides in, a folder that opens — turns a flat screen into something that feels physical. Those small moments add up to a product that feels crafted instead of assembled.

## Why microinteractions matter

Microinteractions are the tiny, purposeful animations that confirm what just happened: a state changed, something loaded, an action was acknowledged. They give the interface a heartbeat. Done well, they make a web app feel as fluid as a native one, which is increasingly the bar people expect.

## Who I learn from

A lot of what I've figured out about motion on the web traces back to a handful of people doing it really well: [Emil Kowalski](https://emilkowal.ski/), [Benji Taylor](https://x.com/benjitaylor), and [Jhey](https://x.com/jh3yy). Their experiments and writeups have shaped how I think about timing, easing, and restraint — and pushed me to build my own.
